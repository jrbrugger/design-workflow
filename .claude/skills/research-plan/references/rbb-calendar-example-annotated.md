# Annotated Example: Calendar Widget Research Brief

This is a research brief for a Calendar Widget project at a regional business bank, annotated with notes on what makes each section strong. Use this as a reference when writing your own briefs.

---

## 1. Project Scope

**Problem:**
SME business owners using the bank's mobile app lack visibility into upcoming financial events (tax deadlines, loan payments, invoice due dates). They currently rely on external calendars, sticky notes, or memory — leading to missed deadlines and late fees.

**Why now:**
Q1 2026 customer feedback surveys showed "financial planning" as the #2 unmet need for SME users. The product team is evaluating a calendar widget for the Q3 roadmap and needs research to validate the concept before committing engineering resources.

**In scope:**
- How SME owners currently track financial deadlines
- Mental models around financial planning and scheduling
- Concept evaluation of a calendar widget in the banking app

**Out of scope:**
- Personal banking users (consumer segment)
- Technical feasibility of calendar integrations
- Visual design of the widget

**Sponsor:** Product Manager, SME Banking

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - Problem is specific and user-centered (not "we want to build a calendar")
> - "Why now" cites real data (Q1 survey) — not vague urgency
> - Scope is tight: 3 things IN, 3 things OUT
> - Named sponsor gives the research organizational backing

---

## 2. Research Goals

1. **Understand** how SME business owners currently track and manage upcoming financial deadlines
2. **Identify** the top pain points and workarounds in their current financial scheduling workflow
3. **Evaluate** whether a calendar widget in the banking app would address their core planning needs
4. **Discover** what information and features SME owners would expect from a financial calendar

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - Exactly 4 goals — focused but comprehensive
> - Action verbs: Understand, Identify, Evaluate, Discover
> - Progressive depth: current state → pain points → concept → expectations
> - No "validate" — this is genuinely exploratory
> - Each goal maps to a clear decision the team needs to make

---

## 3. Target Group

**Primary:** SME business owners (1–50 employees) who use the bank's business banking app at least weekly and manage their own financial administration.

**Secondary:** Bookkeepers or financial administrators who manage finances on behalf of SME owners.

**Recruitment criteria:**
- Active business banking customer
- Business size: 1–50 employees
- Manages own financial deadlines (or delegates to bookkeeper)
- Uses the banking app at least 1x/week
- Mix of industries (retail, services, tech)

**Exclusion criteria:**
- Businesses with dedicated finance departments (>50 employees)
- Users who only use the app for balance checking (not financial planning)

**Sample size:** 8 participants (6 primary, 2 secondary)

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - Defined by behavior ("manage own financial administration") not just demographics
> - Clear primary/secondary split — knows who matters most
> - Recruitment criteria are specific enough to write a screener from
> - Exclusion criteria prevent wasted sessions
> - Sample size is realistic for qualitative research with reasoning

---

## 4. Known Insights

### Facts (with sources)
| Insight | Source | Date |
|---------|--------|------|
| 67% of SME users check the app 3+ times/week | App analytics | Q4 2025 |
| "Financial planning" ranked #2 unmet need in SME segment | Customer survey (n=1,240) | Q1 2026 |
| 34% of support calls relate to missed payment deadlines | Support ticket analysis | 2025 annual |
| Average SME owner manages 12+ recurring financial events/month | Industry benchmark | 2024 |
| Current app has no calendar or scheduling features | Product audit | Q1 2026 |

### Assumptions (to validate)
| Assumption | Based on | Confidence |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| SME owners want financial events in their banking app (not a separate tool) | Survey open-text responses | Medium |
| Users would check a calendar widget at least weekly | Analogy to existing app usage patterns | Low |
| Tax deadlines and loan payments are the most critical event types | Support ticket themes | Medium |
| Users currently use Google Calendar or spreadsheets for tracking | Team intuition | Low |

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - 5 facts, all with sources and dates — this team did their homework
> - Mix of sources: analytics, surveys, support data, benchmarks, audits
> - Assumptions are honest about confidence levels
> - Low-confidence assumptions become research priorities
> - Clear separation — no facts disguised as assumptions or vice versa

---

## 5. Main Research Question

> How do SME business owners currently manage upcoming financial deadlines, and would a calendar widget in the banking app meaningfully improve their financial planning workflow?

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - One sentence, two parts: current state + concept evaluation
> - Starts with "How" — exploratory, not leading
> - "Meaningfully improve" sets a bar — not just "would they use it"
> - Specific enough to answer, broad enough to encompass all sub-questions

---

## 6. Sub-Questions

1. What financial events do SME owners need to track, and how do they currently track them? *(Contextual inquiry)*
2. Where do breakdowns occur in their current financial scheduling workflow? *(Interviews)*
3. What workarounds have they developed for managing deadlines? *(Interviews)*
4. How do they prioritize when multiple financial events compete for attention? *(Interviews)*
5. Would a calendar widget in the banking app fit into their existing workflow? *(Concept testing)*
6. What information would they expect to see on a financial calendar? *(Card sorting / concept testing)*

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - 6 questions — right in the sweet spot
> - Progressive depth: what → where → how → would → what (expectation)
> - Each tagged with a research method
> - Q1–Q4 are about current state; Q5–Q6 are about the concept
> - No leading questions — all neutral phrasing

---

## 7. Hypotheses

1. **We believe** most SME owners use a patchwork of tools (calendar apps, spreadsheets, memory) to track financial deadlines **because** we see high support call volume about missed payments and no dedicated tool in our app.
   **We'll know this is true when** ≥ 5 of 8 participants describe using 2+ tools for financial scheduling.

2. **We believe** tax deadlines and loan payment dates are the highest-priority financial events **because** these have the steepest penalties for missing.
   **We'll know this is true when** participants consistently rank these in their top 3 event types.

3. **We believe** users would engage with a calendar widget at least weekly **because** 67% already check the app 3+ times/week.
   **We'll know this is true when** ≥ 6 of 8 participants say they would check it weekly or more during concept testing.

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - 3 hypotheses — focused and testable
> - Each follows the "believe / because / know when" format
> - Evidence thresholds are specific (5/8, top 3, 6/8)
> - Based on known insights from Section 4
> - Genuinely falsifiable — could be wrong about all three

---

## 8. Research Methods

| Method | Why This Method | Participants | Format |
|--------|----------------|-------------|--------|
| Semi-structured interviews with contextual inquiry | Understand current workflows and pain points (Q1–Q4) | 8 | Remote, 45 min each, moderated |
| Concept testing (lo-fi prototype) | Evaluate calendar widget concept and expectations (Q5–Q6) | Same 8 | Second half of interview session |
| Competitive analysis | Map existing financial calendar/planning tools | 5 competitors | Desk research |

**Research type:** Mixed — Descriptive (current state) + Evaluative (concept)

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - 3 methods, each justified by the questions they answer
> - Efficient design: interviews + concept testing in one 45-min session
> - Competitive analysis adds context without adding participant burden
> - Format details are specific: remote, duration, moderated
> - Research type clearly identified

---

## 9. Test Metrics

### Quantitative
| Metric | Current Baseline | Target | Method |
|--------|-----------------|--------|--------|
| % of users with 2+ tools for financial scheduling | Unknown (hypothesis: ≥ 63%) | Establish baseline | Interview self-report |
| Concept desirability score | N/A | ≥ 4.0 / 5.0 | Post-concept Likert scale |
| % of participants who would use widget weekly | N/A | ≥ 75% (6/8) | Concept testing question |

### Qualitative
- Participants can articulate how the calendar widget fits into their existing workflow
- No critical confusion about what the widget does or shows
- Participants identify at least 3 event types they'd want on the calendar
- Emotional response to concept is positive or constructively critical (not indifferent)

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - Quantitative metrics have specific thresholds (4.0/5.0, 75%)
> - Honest about unknown baselines — research will establish them
> - Qualitative indicators are observable and specific
> - Mix of behavioral (would use), attitudinal (desirability), and comprehension metrics
> - "Not indifferent" is smart — indifference is worse than criticism

## 10. Business Impact

**Business metric supported:** Customer retention + feature adoption (SME segment)

**How findings will be used:**
- Go/no-go decision on calendar widget for Q3 roadmap
- Priority ranking of event types to include in MVP
- Input for design brief (information architecture, content)

**Impact category:** Quality (better financial planning experience) → Retention (reduced churn from missed deadlines)

**Stakeholders who will act on findings:**
- Product Manager, SME Banking — go/no-go decision on widget
- Lead Designer — design brief and concept direction
- Engineering Lead — feasibility scoping based on required integrations
- Head of SME — strategic roadmap alignment

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - Direct connection to business metrics (retention, adoption)
> - Specific decisions listed — not vague "inform strategy"
> - Named stakeholders with their specific roles in acting on findings
> - Impact chain is clear: quality → retention → business value
> - Research is positioned as a decision-making tool, not just "nice to know"

## 11. Planning & Timeline

| Week | Activities | Deliverable | Dependencies |
|------|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| 1 | Finalize brief, write discussion guide, begin recruitment | Approved brief + screener sent | Stakeholder review by Wed |
| 2 | Recruit participants, create lo-fi prototype for concept testing | 8 confirmed participants + prototype | Design team delivers prototype by Fri |
| 2–3 | Conduct 8 interview sessions (4 per week) | Raw notes + recordings | Participant availability |
| 4 | Analysis: affinity mapping, pattern identification, concept scoring | Draft findings document | — |
| 5 | Synthesize insights, write recommendations, prepare presentation | Research report + slide deck | — |
| 6 | Present to stakeholders, hand off to design, archive materials | Presentation delivered + design brief input | Stakeholder calendars |

**Key milestones:**
- Week 1, Friday: Brief approved
- Week 2, Friday: All participants confirmed + prototype ready
- Week 3, Friday: All sessions complete
- Week 5, Friday: Report finalized
- Week 6, Wednesday: Stakeholder presentation

> **✅ What makes this strong:**
> - 6-week timeline is realistic for this scope
> - Dependencies are explicit (stakeholder review, prototype delivery, calendars)
> - Each week has both activities AND deliverables
> - Milestones are date-specific (not "sometime in week 3")
> - Buffer built in: 2 weeks for 8 sessions allows for reschedules

## Summary: Why This Brief Works

| Quality | How This Brief Achieves It |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **Focused** | Tight scope with explicit boundaries |
| **Evidence-based** | Known insights section prevents assumption-driven research |
| **Actionable** | Every section connects to a business decision |
| **Testable** | Hypotheses with thresholds, metrics with numbers |
| **Realistic** | Timeline includes buffer and names dependencies |
| **Stakeholder-ready** | Business impact + named decision-makers |
| **Methodologically sound** | Methods matched to question types |
